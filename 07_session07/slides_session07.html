Introduction

~ 5 minutes

Political parties


Building on Sartori (1976):

A party is any political group identified by an official label that presents at elections, and is capable of placing through elections, candidates for public office

But parties do more than contest elections: they structure political competition and link citizens to the state

  • They aggregate diverse interests into coherent programmes
  • They recruit political elites and socialize them into democratic institutions
  • They organize government and the opposition
  • They mobilize citizens and stimulate political participation

Sartori’s definition is deliberately minimal. The broader functions matter for this session: it is precisely those functions — aggregation, mobilization, representation — that parties have been losing.

Parties and democracy


The birth of representative democracy is inseparable from the emergence of organized factions that structured representatives’ interests and behaviour


Parties and democracy co-evolved: as suffrage expanded, parties became the primary vehicle for translating mass preferences into government


In the 2000s, scholars identified a slippage of parties’ traditional functions

Today, we explore its relationship with democratic backsliding

Modern representative democracy was not built before parties — it was built through them. This session explores the role of the slip of their representative function for democratic backsliding

This session’s goals


  • Understand the concept of party democracy
  • Explore the weakening of party democracy
  • Discuss the relation between populism, the weakening of party democracy, and democratic backsliding
  • Students’ presentation: the case of United States

The weakening of party democracy

~ 15 minutes

Peter Mair


  • Irish political scientist (1951–2011), professor at the European University Institute in Florence

  • Leading scholar of comparative party politics and European democracy

  • Key works: Ruling the Void (2013), the cartel party thesis with Katz (1995), and extensive work on party systems and cleavage structures

Parties as intermediaries

Mair, 2009


Parties have a double function


Representative

Responsive to voters

Accountable to the national constituency

Rooted in civil society

Partisan and ideological

Governmental

Responsible to multiple principals

Accountable to supranational actors (EU, IMF…)

Rooted in the state apparatus

Technocratic and managerial

Party democracy

Mair, 2009


As long as parties fulfilled both functions, Mair spoke of party democracy:

A form of democratic government in which parties are the central vehicle through which democratic life is organized — linking citizens to the state, structuring competition, and making government accountable


Both functions co-evolved with parties themselves — from cadre parties to mass parties, the representative and governmental roles developed together

The evolution of political parties


Party organizational forms have evolved across the 20th century:

  • Cadre/elite parties (19th century): small networks of notables; politics as elite affair
  • Mass parties (1880s–1960s): large membership organizations; strong roots in social cleavages (class, religion); genuine representative function
  • Catch-all parties (1960s–1980s): Kirchheimer (1966); ideological moderation; appeal to broad electorate; declining class/confessional anchoring
  • Cartel parties (1990s–present): Katz & Mair (1995); parties colonize the state; use public resources to survive; collusion between mainstream parties; representation function hollowed out

The cartel party thesis is the immediate precondition for Mair’s later diagnosis. Parties no longer need civil society because they fund themselves through the state.

The hollowing of party democracy

Mair, 2013


A further step beyond the cartel party thesis: “ruling the void”


Parties increasingly prioritize the governmental function at the expense of the representative function:

  • They govern, but no longer represent
  • The space between parties and civil society grows
  • Citizens feel unheard even as parties continue to win elections and form governments

Mair, Ruling the Void (2013, posthumous). Parties continue to govern but the societal foundations of that governance have eroded — hence “the void.” This is the structural diagnosis that motivates the rest of the session.

The empirical evidence


Party membership — Mair & van Biezen (2001); van Biezen, Mair & Poguntke (2012):

  • Dramatic decline across all Western European countries since the 1970s
  • Average membership ratio fell from ~15% to ~5% of the electorate
  • Parties have “all but abandoned any pretensions to being mass organizations”


Party identification — Dalton (2000): “parties without partisans”

  • Systematic decline in party identification across advanced democracies
  • Rise of independent voters and electoral volatility

Van Biezen, Mair & Poguntke (2012) document membership ratios across 22 European countries from the 1960s to the 2000s. Dalton uses survey evidence across democracies. Both converge: organizational and psychological ties between parties and citizens have weakened.

What happens when parties lose their representative role?


  • Dissatisfaction with mainstream parties and political institutions
  • Erosion of confidence in representative democracy as a system
  • Citizens perceive parties as all the same, governing in their own interest, not the public’s

This creates the opportunity for anti-establishment feelings to emerge and for populist parties to exploit them

The populist challenge

~ 10 minutes

What is populism?

Mudde & Kaltwasser, 2017

Following the ideational approach, populism as a thin-centred ideology:

Society is divided into two homogeneous and antagonistic camps — “the pure people” versus “the corrupt elite” — and politics should be an expression of the volonté générale (general will) of the people

Three core concepts:

  • The people: sovereign, common people, or nation — a discursive construction; always unified, always virtuous
  • The elite: defined morally — corrupt, self-serving, and illegitimate; not just political but economic, media, cultural elites
  • The general will: the authentic, unmediated will of the people; politics must express it, not deliberate about it

Populism as a thin ideology

Mudde & Kaltwasser, 2017


Because populism is thin-centred, it is highly malleable:

  • It attaches to very different host ideologies
  • This produces both right-wing populists (Orbán, Le Pen, Trump) and left-wing populists (Chávez, Podemos, Syriza)
  • They share the people/elite frame but define who the people and who the elite are very differently

→ Left populists tend to define the elite economically (oligarchs, corporations)

→ Right populists tend to define the elite culturally (cosmopolitan liberals, media, academics)

The populist challenge

Kriesi, 2017


Building on Mair: the erosion of the representative function of parties creates structural opportunity for populist challengers


Two structural drivers:

  1. Denationalisation of governance — EU and international institutions shift decision-making upward; parties serve multiple principals; accountability to national electorates weakens (but notice contrast with CEE)
  1. Mediatisation of politics — media logic replaces party organisation; leaders communicate directly with citizens; personalised politics replaces programmatic representation

Three forms of populist challenge

Kriesi, 2017


The hollowing of party democracy opens space for three forms of response:

New challengers

Populist parties enter the system and articulate neglected conflicts

FPÖ, RN, Podemos

Anti-politics

Wholesale rejection of the party system as corrupt and illegitimate

M5S (Grillo), Jon Gnarr

Protest expansion

Mobilisation beyond the electoral channel — social movements and direct action

Indignados, Occupy

Populism and democracy

~ 10 minutes

The corrective role of populism

Mudde & Kaltwasser, 2017


Populism can be corrective:

  • It gives voice to groups excluded from mainstream representation
  • It forces mainstream parties to address previously neglected issues
  • Populist challengers may drive realignment, bringing party systems closer to current structural changes in Western societies (Kriesi, 2017)

Whether populism corrects or threatens depends on context: who wields it, the goals it is attached to, and the institutional context

Populism as a threat to democracy


Most often, however, populism is framed as a threat to democracy

Populism builds on a majoritarian view of democracy that can justify backsliding behaviour:

  • Checks and balances are elite-created obstacles to the popular will
  • Independent courts and media are instruments of the corrupt elite
  • Minority rights are dismissed in favour of majoritarian homogeneity
  • Intermediaries — parties, parliament — are suspect; a direct leader-people link is preferred

As a result, some view populism as a pathway to autocracy (e.g. Kendall-Taylor & Frantz, 2016)

Populism & executive aggrandizement

Bessen, 2024


Research question: Does populist discourse increase citizen support for executive aggrandizement?

Argument: Populism increases support for executive aggrandizement by:

  1. Framing the president as the sole legitimate representative of the people

  2. Framing institutional opposition as corrupt elite obstruction

This makes power grabs look democratic and legitimate

Observational & experimental evidence

Bessen, 2024


Study 1: Cross-national LAPOP surveys, 18 Latin American countries, 2010–2019 (~81,000 respondents); three-level multilevel logistic regression

  • Outcome: support for president closing congress / supreme court

Study 2: Survey experiment in Ecuador with a 3×2 factorial design manipulating mayors’ justification to close the municipal council

  • Outcome: support for closing municipal congress

Findings: populism increases support for backsliding

Bessen, 2024


Study 1: more populist presidential discourse → higher support for closing congress; effect largest among presidential supporters

Study 2: populist and anti-elitist frames both significantly increase support for closing the legislature vs. baseline


Implication: politicians can reshape public opinion, justifying anti-democratic actions on the basis of populist arguments

Conclusion

~ 5 minutes

Summary


  • Party democracy is a form of democratic government in which parties fulfill both a representative and a governmental function, linking citizens to the state and making government accountable
  • Party democracy has eroded: declining membership, identification, and representative function, driven by denationalisation of governance and mediatisation
  • This creates structural opportunity for populists, who carry a thin-centred ideology rooted in a majoritarian and illiberal view of democracy
  • Once in government, populist politicians can engage in backsliding processes — using popular mandates to dismantle the very institutions that constrain executive power
